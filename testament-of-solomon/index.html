The Testament of Solomon

The King’s Book of Demons
Written 1st–5th century AD. Condemned repeatedly as diabolical. The source of Western ceremonial magic’s entire system of demonic classification and binding.

The Testament of Solomon claims to record how King Solomon built the Temple in Jerusalem: not with human labour alone, but by summoning and binding seventy-two demons, compelling each one to work on the Temple and reveal the angel whose name could bind them. One by one, the demons appear before Solomon’s ring, confess their nature, describe the diseases and calamities they cause, and are put to work. The result is both a demonological encyclopaedia and a manual of spiritual warfare.

This text is the direct ancestor of the Goetia, the Ars Notoria, and the entire Western tradition of ceremonial magic. The Church condemned it as diabolical while simultaneously absorbing its demonological framework. The list of 72 demons in the Testament became the 72 demons of the Lesser Key of Solomon, which is still in print. The source was suppressed. The derivative tradition flourished.

“I Solomon glorified the Lord God, and then I asked of the demon: Who art thou, and what is thy name? And he said: I am called Ornias.”

Solomon built the Temple with demonic labour. This is his record of how.
