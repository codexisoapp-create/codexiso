The Clementine Recognitions

Complete Edition
3rd–4th century. The largest and most complex of the early Christian apocrypha. Presents Peter and Simon Magus in sustained theological debate. Contains proto-orthodox theology and Ebionite elements.

The Clementine Recognitions is a novel-length early Christian text narrating how Clement of Rome — the third or fourth bishop of Rome after Peter — joined Peter’s mission, followed him in his debates with Simon Magus, and eventually discovered that his long-lost family had all converted independently and were scattered across the Mediterranean. The “recognitions” of the title are the family reunions that conclude the text.

The theological content is rich and complex: a sustained account of Peter’s theology that emphasises the True Prophet who has appeared in every age from Adam onwards, a critique of astrological determinism, and a series of debates between Peter and Simon Magus that touch on every major theological question of the 2nd and 3rd centuries. The text preserves traces of Ebionite Jewish-Christian theology that the later Catholic tradition suppressed.

“The True Prophet has warned us that our God is one, and that He alone is to be worshipped, and that we should not set up many gods, in accordance with the mischievous and deceiving doctrine of the Greeks.”
