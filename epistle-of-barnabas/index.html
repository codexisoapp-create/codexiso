The Epistle of Barnabas

Complete Edition
Written circa 70–130 AD. Included in the Codex Sinaiticus as canonical scripture. Removed from the Bible by the Council of Laodicea, 364 AD.

The Epistle of Barnabas is not an epistle and was not written by Barnabas. It is an anonymous 2nd-century theological treatise that was so highly regarded in early Christianity that it was included as canonical scripture in the Codex Sinaiticus — one of the oldest and most authoritative manuscripts of the Bible in existence. Then it was cut.

The letter argues that the Jews misread their own scriptures from the beginning — that the Law was always meant to be understood spiritually, not literally, and that Israel never actually had a true covenant with God because they broke it when Moses descended from Sinai. This radical supersessionism threatened both Jewish-Christian relations and the theological coherence the Church was trying to construct. It was removed from the canon at the Council of Laodicea in 364 AD.

“The covenant was given to us; but they lost it forever in this way, when Moses had just received it.”

It was in your Bible. They took it out.
