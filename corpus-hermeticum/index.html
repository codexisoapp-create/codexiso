The Corpus Hermeticum

The Hermetic Writings of Trismegistus
Written 1st–3rd century AD. Preserved by Islamic scholars when Europe burned its libraries. Suppressed by the Church as pagan magic. Rediscovered in Florence, 1460.

The Corpus Hermeticum is the foundational text of the Western esoteric tradition. Attributed to Hermes Trismegistus — Thrice-Greatest Hermes, identified with the Egyptian god Thoth — it presents a series of dialogues about the nature of God, the mind, and the cosmos that were believed in the Renaissance to be of immense antiquity, perhaps older than Moses. Cosimo de’ Medici was so impatient to read it when a manuscript arrived in Florence in 1460 that he ordered Ficino to translate it before completing Plato.

The Church had long regarded the Hermetic texts with deep suspicion — not as false, but as dangerous competitors. When Isaac Casaubon demonstrated in 1614 that the texts were written in the 1st–3rd centuries AD rather than in the time of Moses, the texts were largely dismissed as pagan forgeries. But the tradition they embodied — the unity of all religious wisdom, the divinity of the human mind, the possibility of direct mystical union with God — never disappeared.
