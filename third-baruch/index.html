3 Baruch

The Greek Apocalypse of Baruch
1st–3rd century. Jeremiah's secretary Baruch tours the five heavens and discovers the fate of the fallen angels, the origin of the vine, and where the souls of the righteous and wicked go after death.

3 Baruch is an apocalypse in the tradition of Enoch: the scribe Baruch, weeping over the destruction of Jerusalem by Nebuchadnezzar, is guided by an angel through five heavens and shown the secrets of the cosmos. He sees the builders of the Tower of Babel transformed into animals; he sees the Phoenix bird that flies before the sun, carrying its light on its wings; he watches the angels bring the prayers of the righteous to God in golden and silver vessels; and he learns that the vine — the source of Noah's drunkenness, the tree that caused the fall according to this tradition — was planted by the fallen angel Samael himself.

The text survived in Greek and Slavonic manuscripts, largely because it was copied by scribes who found it harmless. Its picture of the heavens is rich and strange: the sun moves in a chariot drawn by angels, the moon's phases depend on the sins of humanity, and the souls of the righteous rest in the third heaven while the wicked are detained. No institutional authority found it important enough to actively suppress — which is perhaps why it survived without the prestige of canonical status.

"And I, Baruch, said: What is this bird? And the angel said: This is the guardian of the world. When the sun goes forth in the morning, this bird stretches its wings and receives the fiery rays of the sun."

Five heavens. The bird that carries the sun. The angel who planted the vine.
