The Ascension of Isaiah

Vision & Martyrdom
2nd century BC–2nd century AD. Read as canonical in some early churches. Contains an account of Christ’s descent through the seven heavens unlike anything in the Bible.

The Ascension of Isaiah is a composite text in two parts: a Jewish martyrdom account in which the prophet Isaiah is sawn in two on the orders of King Manasseh, and a Christian vision in which Isaiah ascends through the seven heavens and witnesses the entire life of Jesus before it happens — the descent, the incarnation, the crucifixion, and the resurrection — as a cosmic drama played out in the heavenly realms.

The vision section presents a remarkable proto-Gnostic cosmology in which Christ descends through the seven heavens in disguise, taking on the form of each realm’s angels so as not to be recognised by the demonic powers. The crucifixion is a consequence of the Prince of this World’s ignorance. This docetic, esoteric reading of the Incarnation was incompatible with orthodox theology and the text was sidelined, though it was read as authoritative in some early communities.

“And I saw One standing whose glory surpassed that of all, and His glory was great and wonderful, and after I had looked at Him, all the righteous whom I had seen and also the angels whom I had seen came to Him.”

Isaiah saw the entire life of Christ before it happened. The Church decided you shouldn’t know.
