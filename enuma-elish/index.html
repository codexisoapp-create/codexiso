The Enuma Elish

The Babylonian Creation Epic
Written circa 18th–12th century BC. The Babylonian creation myth that predates Genesis by over a thousand years and shares its structure. Suppressed by Biblical scholars for a century after discovery.

When George Smith of the British Museum read Tablet XI of the Epic of Gilgamesh to the Society of Biblical Archaeology in London in 1872, he had discovered a Babylonian flood story — complete with ark, birds, and dove — that was older than Genesis. The room fell silent. The discovery triggered a crisis in Biblical scholarship that has never fully resolved. The Enuma Elish, the Babylonian creation epic, deepened that crisis: here was a creation narrative written a thousand years before Genesis that shared its structure, its sequence, and even some of its language.

The academic and religious establishment’s response was suppression and dismissal: the Babylonian texts were derivatives, corruptions, pagan borrowings from the original Hebrew truth. This position, maintained against mounting evidence for a century, has now been entirely abandoned. The consensus of modern scholarship is that Genesis drew on and transformed Mesopotamian traditions rather than the reverse. The texts they didn’t want you to read first were, in fact, first.

“When on high the heaven had not been named, firm ground below had not been called by name; naught but primordial Apsu, their begetter, and Mummu-Tiamat, she who bore them all.”

Written a thousand years before Genesis. Sharing Genesis’s structure. Draw your own conclusions.
