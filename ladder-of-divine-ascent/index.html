The Ladder of Divine Ascent

John Climacus
7th century. Thirty steps to mystical union with God, written by a monk who lived forty years in solitude. The most influential text of Eastern Christian mysticism. Suppressed in the West at the Reformation as "works righteousness."

The Ladder of Divine Ascent was written around 600 AD by John, the abbot of the monastery at the foot of Mount Sinai — a man later called John Climacus ("of the Ladder"). It describes thirty ascending steps of the spiritual life, from renunciation of the world through the virtues of the active life to the heights of contemplation, prayer, and divine love. For fourteen centuries it has been read aloud in Eastern Orthodox monasteries during Lent. It is the most influential guide to mystical life in Christian history outside the New Testament itself.

The Protestant Reformation suppressed the Ladder of Divine Ascent not through condemnation but through indifference: its entire theology — that spiritual growth requires sustained practice, stages of development, ascetic discipline, and contemplative prayer — was incompatible with the Protestant doctrine of grace alone. If salvation is entirely God's act and requires nothing from the human side, then a thirty-step program of spiritual development is either unnecessary or heretical. The Ladder was therefore quietly dropped from the Western Christian tradition, surviving in the East while the West forgot it.

"A monk is a violence done to nature and a wrestling with the senses. A monk is continual worship of God and service before God."

Thirty steps to God. The West stopped climbing at the Reformation.
