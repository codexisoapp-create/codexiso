Thunder, Perfect Mind

Complete Edition
Written 2nd–4th century. Suppressed with the Nag Hammadi library circa 390 AD. Unique in the ancient world — a goddess speaking in her own voice.

Thunder, Perfect Mind is unlike any other text in the ancient world. It is a poem — a series of paradoxical proclamations spoken by a feminine divine voice who declares herself simultaneously virgin and mother, wife and daughter, bride and bridegroom, barren and fertile, the ignorant and the wise. She is the first and the last. She is the whore and the holy one. Nothing can contain her in a single definition.

For a religion in the process of defining and delimiting the divine — one God, male, absolute, mediated through male priests and bishops — this text was a catastrophe. The divine feminine speaking in her own voice, refusing category, exceeding doctrine? The text was ordered buried. The Church suppressed not just the content but the entire mode of experiencing the sacred that it represents.

Scholars have no definitive explanation for what Thunder, Perfect Mind is or where it came from. It has no narrative, no doctrine, no theology. It is pure voice. It has been read aloud at poetry festivals. It has been called the most powerful text in the Nag Hammadi library.

“I am the first and the last. I am the honoured one and the scorned one. I am the whore and the holy one. I am the wife and the virgin.”

She was not lost. She was buried. She is speaking again.
