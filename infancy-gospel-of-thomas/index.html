The Infancy Gospel of Thomas

The Childhood of Jesus
Written circa 150–200 AD. Condemned by the Decretum Gelasianum. Shows a Jesus who is terrifying, wilful, and divine before he is good — a portrait the Church could not accept.

The Infancy Gospel of Thomas presents the child Jesus as a being of terrifying divine power who has not yet learned restraint. When a boy accidentally bumps into him, Jesus curses him and he dies. When a teacher tries to educate him, Jesus humiliates him before the whole synagogue. When another child breaks his water jar, Jesus miraculously fills his cloak with water instead — but when a child destroys his clay sparrows, Jesus brings them to life and they fly away forever.

This Jesus is recognisable to anyone who has thought carefully about what it would actually mean for a divine being to inhabit a child’s body. He is capricious, jealous of his dignity, astonishing. He is not the gentle Jesus of Sunday school. The text was condemned not for being false but for being too honest about the implications of the Incarnation.

“When Jesus was five years old he was playing at the crossing of a stream, and he gathered together the waters that flowed there into pools, and made them immediately pure.”

The canonical Gospels skip from the manger to the Jordan. This is what they omitted.
