The Gospel of Bartholomew

Questions of Bartholomew
4th–5th century. Condemned by the Decretum Gelasianum as apocryphal. Contains conversations with the Devil and descriptions of hell absent from canonical scripture.

The Gospel of Bartholomew contains one of the most extraordinary scenes in all apocryphal literature: the apostles, after the resurrection, ask Jesus to show them where he went during the three days between the cross and the empty tomb. He shows them — hell, Hades, the underworld. And then Mary is asked to describe the moment of the Incarnation: how the Word entered her body. Her account is so overwhelming that the apostles prostrate themselves, unable to bear it.

Later, Satan himself is brought before the apostles in chains and forced to answer their questions. He explains the hierarchy of demons, the nature of sin, and how the fallen angels operate. This is not the sanitised demonology of orthodoxy but a full, specific, terrifying alternative theology of evil. The text was condemned by the Decretum Gelasianum — the list of apocryphal books issued under Pope Gelasius I around 495 AD.

“And Bartholomew saith unto him: Tell us, thou that art concealed in the abyss of hell, what is the darkness, and what the dwelling-place of thy habitation?”

Satan testified before the apostles. The Church decided you shouldn’t hear it.
