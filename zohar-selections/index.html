The Zohar

The Book of Radiance — Selected Portions
Composed 13th century Spain by Moses de Leon, attributed to 2nd century Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai. The central text of Kabbalah. Banned by rabbinical authorities multiple times.

The Zohar — the Book of Radiance — is the central text of Kabbalah: a multi-volume mystical commentary on the Torah presented as the teachings of the 2nd-century Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, dictated in a cave where he hid for thirteen years. Modern scholarship attributes its composition to Moses de Leon of Guadalajara, writing in 13th-century Spain. Whether ancient or medieval, it is one of the most extraordinary religious texts ever written: a universe of mystical interpretation in which every word of Scripture conceals multiple layers of cosmic meaning.

The Zohar was controversial from its first appearance. Rabbinical authorities debated whether to suppress it, and some did. The Spanish Inquisition recognised it as the source of messianic Judaism and targeted its readers. When Sabbatai Zevi declared himself the Messiah in 1665 and then converted to Islam, the rabbinical establishment blamed Kabbalistic Messianism and attempted to suppress the Zohar again. It survived because it was already too central to Jewish mysticism to eradicate.
