The Syriac Apocalypse of Baruch

2 Baruch — Complete Edition
Written circa 70–100 AD after the destruction of Jerusalem. A response to catastrophe that the rabbis found too apocalyptic to canonise.

2 Baruch is a response to one of the most traumatic events in Jewish history: the destruction of the Temple by the Romans in 70 AD. Baruch, the secretary of the prophet Jeremiah, is left behind while the rest of the community is taken into exile. He sits by the ruins of Jerusalem and weeps. Then God speaks to him, and what follows is a remarkable meditation on suffering, divine justice, and the hope of resurrection.

The rabbis who fixed the Hebrew canon around the end of the 1st century AD were cautious about apocalyptic texts. The destruction of the Temple had produced a flowering of visionary literature — 2 Baruch, 4 Ezra, the Apocalypse of Abraham — that expressed the community’s grief and its hope for God’s intervention. The rabbis chose to fold that energy into the Talmudic tradition rather than preserve it in scripture. 2 Baruch was written in Syriac and survived in the Eastern Christian tradition.
