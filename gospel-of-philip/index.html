The Gospel of Philip

Nag Hammadi Edition
Buried at Nag Hammadi circa 390 AD following the canonisation edicts of Theophilus of Alexandria. Lost for 1,550 years.

The Gospel of Philip is a Valentinian Gnostic text from the 2nd or 3rd century — a collection of reflections, sayings, and theological meditations on the sacraments of the early Gnostic church. It describes five sacred rites: baptism, chrism, eucharist, redemption, and the bridal chamber. That last sacrament — a ritual of spiritual union — is why the Church found it intolerable.

Philip names Mary Magdalene as the companion of Jesus, the one he loved more than his other disciples, and whom he kissed often on the mouth. For the orthodox Church, this portrait of a spiritually intimate, sexually ambiguous relationship between Jesus and a woman was incompatible with the celibate, hierarchical theology they were constructing. The text was ordered destroyed in the late 4th century.

It survived only because Egyptian monks buried their library in the desert rather than burn it. When the jar was unsealed in 1945, Philip emerged intact, carrying a vision of sacred marriage that had been hidden from Christianity for fifteen centuries.

“The companion of the Saviour is Mary Magdalene. Christ loved her more than all the disciples and used to kiss her often.”

Fifteen centuries of silence, ended by a jar of sand.
