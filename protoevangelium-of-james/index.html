The Protoevangelium of James

The Birth of Mary
Written circa 145–200 AD. Source of most Catholic doctrine about Mary (perpetual virginity, Immaculate Conception). Condemned by Pope Gelasius 495 AD.

Here is the paradox at the heart of the Protoevangelium: the Catholic Church condemned this text as apocryphal while simultaneously basing some of its most important Marian doctrines on it. The perpetual virginity of Mary — that she remained a virgin after giving birth to Jesus — rests almost entirely on this text, which includes the famous scene of a midwife physically examining Mary after the delivery and finding her intact. The names of Mary’s parents, Joachim and Anne, come from this text alone.

Pope Gelasius I condemned it in 495 AD because it contradicted the canonical gospels on specific points and contained legendary material unsuitable for liturgical use. Yet its influence on Christian iconography, doctrine, and devotion has been profound. Millions of Christians celebrate the feast of Saints Joachim and Anne without knowing their names come from a text declared apocryphal fifteen centuries ago.

“And behold, an angel of the Lord stood by her, and said, Anne, Anne, the Lord hath heard thy prayer, and thou shalt conceive, and shall bring forth; and thy seed shall be spoken of in all the world.”

The Church condemned this book while teaching everything it says.
