Confessio Fraternitatis

The Second Rosicrucian Manifesto
Published Kassel 1615. Companion text to the Fama. More explicitly alchemical and anti-Papal than its predecessor. Immediately condemned by Jesuit authorities.

The Confessio Fraternitatis is more explicit than the Fama and more aggressive. It announces a coming universal reformation — a transformation of all knowledge, all religion, and all politics that is already underway. It attacks the Pope as the Antichrist. It claims the Brotherhood possesses the Universal Medicine and the Philosopher’s Stone. It invites all learned men to respond.

The Jesuit response was immediate and furious. The Inquisition classified the text as seditious. Protestant authorities were barely more welcoming — the Confessio’s alchemy and Hermetism sat uneasily with Reformation theology. Yet the text circulated everywhere, found its way into every intellectual circle in Europe, and helped create the mental world from which the Scientific Revolution and the Enlightenment emerged.

“We do testify that after a time there will now be a general reformation both of divine and human things, according to our desire, and the expectation of others.”

The Jesuits condemned it. The Scientific Revolution followed anyway.
