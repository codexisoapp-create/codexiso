The Damascus Document

Dead Sea Scrolls
Written 2nd–1st century BC. Fragments found in the Cairo Geniza 1896, complete text at Qumran 1947. Describes the Teacher of Righteousness and the sect’s theology of history.

The Damascus Document describes the founding of the Qumran sect: a group who separated from mainstream Judaism under a mysterious leader called the Teacher of Righteousness, opposed by an equally mysterious adversary called the Man of Lies. The community went into exile, understood themselves as the true Israel, and awaited the coming of two Messiahs — one priestly, one royal. They also had specific expectations about a prophetic figure called the Interpreter of the Law.

The parallels with the New Testament are extensive and were among the most carefully suppressed findings of the Dead Sea Scrolls scholarship. A community that believed in the imminent end of the world, led by a Teacher of Righteousness persecuted by religious authorities, awaiting two Messiahs — all of this predates and contextualises the origins of Christianity in ways that challenge traditional accounts of Christian uniqueness.

“Listen now, all you who know righteousness, and consider the works of God; for He has a dispute with all flesh and will condemn all those who despise Him.”

The Teacher of Righteousness existed before Jesus. Here is his community’s law.
